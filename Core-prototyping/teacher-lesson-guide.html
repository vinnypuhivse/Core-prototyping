Key lesson vocabulary

Argument
A claim supported by reasons and evidence to persuade an audience
Claim
A statement that expresses a writer's position on an issue
Evidence
Facts, examples, or expert opinions that support a claim
Counterclaim
An opposing viewpoint that challenges the main argument
Reasoning
The logical connection between evidence and the claim
Persuasion
The act of convincing someone to accept a particular point of view

Essential questions

1
What makes an argument effective and persuasive?
2
How do writers use evidence to support their claims?
3
Why is it important to address counterclaims in argumentative writing?
4
How can we evaluate the credibility of sources and evidence?

Lesson outline / sequence

1
Introduction & Hook
5 min

Begin with an engaging scenario or question that introduces students to the concept of argumentation. Present a relatable topic where students might have differing opinions to spark discussion.

Choose an approach:
Option A Class Debate Starter

Present a controversial topic (e.g., "Should students have longer recess?") and have students stand on different sides of the room based on their opinion.

Option B Think-Pair-Share

Students individually think about a question, discuss with a partner, then share their reasoning with the class to highlight different perspectives.

Option C Video Hook

Show a short video clip that presents a persuasive argument. Ask students to identify what made the argument convincing.

2
Direct Instruction
10 min

Explicitly teach the key components of argumentative writing. Use clear examples and visual aids to help students understand how claims, evidence, and reasoning work together.

Choose an approach:
Option A Anchor Chart Creation

Create an anchor chart with students showing the structure of an argument (Claim ‚Üí Evidence ‚Üí Reasoning). Use a familiar example.

Option B Annotated Model Text

Display a model argumentative paragraph and annotate it together, identifying and color-coding each component.

Option C Interactive Presentation

Use slides or interactive whiteboard to present concepts with embedded questions and real-time student participation.

3
Guided Practice
15 min

Work together as a class to identify and analyze arguments. Guide students through the process of recognizing claims and evaluating the strength of evidence.

Choose an approach:
Option A Newsela Article Analysis

Read a Newsela article together and have students identify the claim, evidence, and reasoning in small groups.

Option B Claim Sort Activity

Provide students with statements and have them categorize as strong claims, weak claims, or not claims at all.

Option C Evidence Evaluation

Present a claim with multiple pieces of evidence (strong and weak) and discuss which evidence best supports the claim and why.

4
Independent Practice
12 min

Students work independently to apply what they've learned. This is an opportunity for formative assessment and to identify students who may need additional support.

5
Closing & Assessment
3 min

Wrap up the lesson by reviewing key concepts and assessing student understanding. Preview the next lesson and how today's learning will connect to future work.
